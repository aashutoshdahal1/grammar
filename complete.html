Introduction

Welcome!

These lessons will help you learn essential English grammar. When you have a good strong foundation of basic grammar, then you can avoid simple mistakes and progress to higher levels of English.

The topics we will cover include:

• The main verb tenses used in English

• Important words like articles, possessives, and helping verbs

• Adjectives and adverbs

• Sentence structure

Try to do one lesson every day, and make sure to do the quizzes so that you can review and practice what you are learning.

Lesson 2

There is / There are

Structure

We use there is and there are to talk about things that exist and where things are located. Use there is for one thing, and there are for multiple things.

SingularPlural
(+)There is / There's a bed in the room.There are two pillows on the bed.
(–)There isn't a mirror in the bathroom.There aren't any windows.
(?)Is there a table? Yes, there is. / No, there isn't.Are there any chairs? Yes, there are.
Important Note When a list starts with a singular item → use there is.
When a list starts with a plural item → use there are.
Ex: "There is a banana and two apples in the bowl." / "There are two apples and a banana in the bowl."

Present Perfect

Perfect Tense

Formed with have / has + past participle. Used in two main situations:

1) Past experiences (unspecified time): Have you been to Italy? Yes, I've been there three times.

2) Actions that started in the past and continue now: I've been married for five years. / He's been a teacher since last January.

SubjectAuxiliaryPast Participle
I / you / we / theyhave (I've / you've…)been to Paris.
he / she / ithas (he's / she's…)been to Paris.

Lesson 24

Direct & Indirect Objects

Structure

A direct object is the thing acted upon by the verb. Keep the verb and direct object together — don't put other words between them.

I bought yesterday a new t-shirt.

I bought a new t-shirt yesterday.

An indirect object is the person who receives the action. It can go between verb and direct object (without "to"), or at the end (with "to").

I gave John the books. (indirect object in middle)

I gave the books to John. (indirect object at end with "to")

I gave to John the books.
